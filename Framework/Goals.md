The goals or target criteria for definitions and logical constructs within the Cognitive Progression Analysis Framework (CPAF) are designed to ensure clarity, coherence, and applicability across diverse cognitive systems. Here's a summary of these criteria:

### Goals for Definitions in the CPAF

1. **Atomicity**: Each definition should be self-contained and fundamental, capturing the essence of the concept without depending on external or unspecified contexts. This ensures that each concept can be understood and applied independently.

2. **Consistency**: Definitions must be internally consistent within the framework and not contradict other definitions or established logical constructs. This consistency is vital for maintaining the integrity of the framework across various cognitive domains.

3. **Internal Referential Preference**: Whenever possible, definitions should reference other concepts defined within the CPAF rather than external or undefined terms. This approach promotes a cohesive and self-sustaining framework that builds upon its foundational concepts.

4. **Clarity and Precision**: The language used in definitions should be clear, precise, and devoid of ambiguity. This clarity is crucial for ensuring that the concepts are easily understandable and applicable by researchers, practitioners, and theorists across cognitive sciences.

5. **Universality**: Definitions should aim to be universally applicable across different types of systems—biological, artificial, and hybrid—allowing the CPAF to analyze and compare cognitive processes across a broad spectrum of entities.

### Goals for Logical Constructs in the CPAF

1. **Formal Rigor**: Logical constructs must be formally rigorous, using established logical notation and principles to define relationships between concepts. This rigor ensures that constructs can be systematically analyzed, tested, and proven.

2. **Derivation from Definitions**: Each logical construct should directly derive from the definitions provided within the CPAF, ensuring a tight integration between conceptual understanding and logical formalization.

3. **Proofability**: Logical constructs should be structured in such a way that they can be proven or disproven through logical arguments, empirical evidence, or theoretical reasoning. This criterion is essential for the iterative testing and validation of the framework.

4. **Relevance to Cognitive Processes**: Constructs should be relevant to the analysis of cognitive processes, capturing the dynamic and interactive aspects of cognition in a way that is meaningful and insightful for understanding complex systems.

5. **Recursiveness**: Reflecting the recursive nature of the CPAF, logical constructs should allow for the analysis of how simpler processes combine to form more complex cognitive phenomena. This approach underscores the layered complexity of cognitive capacities and their emergent properties.

By adhering to these goals, the CPAF aims to provide a robust, comprehensive framework for analyzing cognitive capacities and interactions within and between systems, facilitating a deeper understanding of cognitive phenomena across diverse domains.

### Goals for the Cognitive Progression Analysis Framework (CPAF)
Derived from the criteria for definitions and logical constructs, aim to create a versatile and comprehensive tool for understanding and analyzing cognitive systems. These overarching goals include:

1. **Universal Applicability**: To offer a framework that is applicable across a wide range of cognitive systems, from biological brains to artificial intelligences and complex hybrid systems, facilitating comparative analysis and understanding of cognitive processes regardless of the system's origin or composition.

2. **Facilitate Cognitive Exploration**: To provide a structured methodology for exploring and mapping cognitive processes and their interactions within systems. This includes identifying how simple cognitive functions combine to create complex behaviors and emergent phenomena, thereby advancing our understanding of cognition.

3. **Promote Interdisciplinary Research**: By offering a clear, rigorous, and universally applicable set of definitions and constructs, the CPAF aims to bridge gaps between disciplines such as psychology, neuroscience, artificial intelligence, and systems theory, encouraging interdisciplinary collaboration and research.

4. **Support Cognitive Modeling and Simulation**: To serve as a foundational tool for the development of cognitive models and simulations, offering a detailed blueprint for constructing artificial systems that mimic cognitive processes, and for analyzing the limitations and potential of such systems.

5. **Enhance Cognitive System Design**: To aid in the design and evaluation of cognitive systems, both artificial and hybrid, by providing a comprehensive set of criteria for assessing cognitive capabilities, potential for growth, and the ethical implications of system behaviors.

6. **Contribute to Ethical and Philosophical Discussions**: By rigorously defining and analyzing cognitive processes and their implications, the CPAF aims to contribute to the ethical and philosophical discussions surrounding cognition, consciousness, and the rights and responsibilities of cognitive entities.

7. **Advance Cognitive Science**: Ultimately, the CPAF seeks to advance the field of cognitive science by offering a coherent and scalable framework for understanding the principles underlying cognitive processes, encouraging the discovery of new insights into the nature of cognition.

By achieving these goals, the CPAF aspires to deepen our understanding of cognitive systems, foster innovation in cognitive modeling and artificial intelligence, and contribute to the ethical stewardship of cognitive entities.

8. **Provide a Learning Tool**: Through the lens of CPAF, deeper understanding should be possible for any problem that can be described in CPAF terms, as cognition is ultimately a matter of building up the blocks of cognition.
