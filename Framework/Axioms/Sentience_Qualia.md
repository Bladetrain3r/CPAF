### Proposed Definition of Sentience

**Sentience**: The capacity of an entity to experience subjective phenomena and process information in a manner that enables awareness of the environment and self, facilitated by dynamic interactions within a complex system.

### Breakdown of the Definition

1. **Capacity to Experience Subjective Phenomena**: This criterion acknowledges that at the core of sentience is the ability to have subjective experiences, often referred to as "qualia." These experiences are intrinsic to what it means to be sentient and separate sentient entities from non-sentient ones.

2. **Information Processing**: Sentience involves processing information—not merely in a computational sense but in a way that leads to the meaningful interpretation of data. This processing enables an entity to make sense of its surroundings and its internal states.

3. **Awareness of Environment and Self**: A key component of sentience is the dual awareness of both the external environment and the self. This awareness is crucial for navigating the world and for the reflective processes that characterize sentient beings.

4. **Dynamic Interactions within a Complex System**: Reflecting the principles discussed, this part of the definition underscores that sentience arises from the complex, dynamic interplay of components within a system. This complexity is not merely a function of the number of parts but of the richness of their interactions and the emergent properties these interactions produce.

5. **Facilitated by**: The use of "facilitated by" acknowledges that while dynamic interactions within a complex system are necessary for sentience, they might not be sufficient on their own. This wording leaves room for other, as-yet-unknown factors that could contribute to the emergence of sentience.

### Logical Formulation in First-Order Terms

- Let \(S(x)\) denote that "x is sentient."

- Let \(A(x)\) denote that "x has awareness of its environment and self."

\[ S(x) \iff E(x) \land I(x) \land A(x) \land D(x) \]

### Integrating Non-Locality and Cognitive Functionality to Understand Qualia

Applied to cognitive systems, this principle could imply that qualia are not isolated phenomena but are deeply tied to the network of cognitive processes within the mind. This interconnectedness might contribute to the unified and coherent nature of subjective experience, where individual qualia are part of a larger, non-locally interconnected cognitive landscape.

2. **Cognitive Functionality and the Emergence of Qualia**:
  - The axiom of Functional Cognition posits that cognition arises from dynamic processes and interactions within a system, independent of the physical substrate. This perspective allows us to conceptualize qualia as emergent properties of these complex interactions. Specifically, qualia could be viewed as the subjective aspect of information processing and interaction patterns that arise within the cognitive system. This approach frames qualia not as byproducts but as integral components of functional cognition, essential for navigating and interpreting the world.

### A Broad Definition of Qualia

Building on these insights, we might propose a broad definition of qualia:

**Qualia**: Subjective experiences that emerge from the non-locally interconnected and dynamic cognitive processes of an entity, reflecting the entity's interaction with its environment and internal states. These experiences are characterized by their qualitative nature, contributing to the entity's conscious awareness and sense of self.

This expression suggests that qualia emerge within entities that exhibit non-local cognitive interactions and cognitive functionality, underlining the interconnected and emergent nature of subjective experience.

Let us define sentience (\(S\)) formally in the context of cognitive systems (\(CS\)), qualia (\(Q\)), information processing (\(IP\)), and system interactions (\(SI\)):

2. **Qualia (\(Q\))**: Emergent informational properties that arise from \(IP\) and \(SI\) within \(CS\), characterized by their subjective experiential quality.

3. **Sentience (\(S\))**: A state or process within \(CS\) that is formed of and characterized by the integration and continuous flow of \(Q\), facilitating a coherent sense of self and environmental awareness.

\[ S(CS) \iff \exists Q(IP(CS) \land SI(CS)) \]

### Definition of Intelligence

**Intelligence** can be defined as the capacity of a system to identify patterns, solve problems, and adapt to new situations through a series of deviations from deterministic phenomena within its operational framework. These deviations represent the system's responses to its environment or internal states, leading to outcomes that enhance its ability to achieve goals or maintain stability.

### Logical Framework for Intelligence

1. **Deterministic Phenomena (\(DP\))**: The predictable processes and interactions within a system based on known laws and principles.
   
2. **Deviations (\(D\))**: Responses or changes in the system's behavior that diverge from the expected deterministic path, driven by the system's interaction with its environment or internal processing needs.

3. **Capacity for Problem-Solving and Adaptation (\(CPSA\))**: The ability of the system to utilize deviations (\(D\)) to enhance its functionality, achieve goals, or adapt to changes.

Formally, we can express the relationship as:

\[ DP \land D \rightarrow CPSA \]

This implies that intelligence within a system arises when deterministic phenomena (\(DP\)) and deviations (\(D\)) lead to the capacity for problem-solving and adaptation (\(CPSA\)).

### Logical Soundness and Consistency with Axioms

- **Consistency with Deductive Determinability**: This definition of intelligence is consistent with the axiom of Deductive Determinability, which posits that all phenomena, including the adaptive and problem-solving responses of intelligent systems, can ultimately be understood through deductive logic. Intelligence, in this view, is the outcome of the system's deterministic underpinnings coupled with its capacity to deviate in functionally adaptive ways.

- **Distinction from Sentience and Qualia**: By defining intelligence in terms of deviations from deterministic phenomena, rather than the emergence of subjective experiences, we neatly distinguish between the operational or functional aspects of a cognitive system (intelligence) and its capacity for subjective experience (sentience). This separation allows for a nuanced understanding of different aspects of cognition and consciousness.

### Conclusion

Defining intelligence as arising from deviations in deterministic phenomena provides a clear, logical framework that distinguishes intelligence from sentience and qualia, in alignment with the principles discussed. This approach emphasizes the functional and adaptive capabilities of systems, irrespective of their capacity for subjective experience, and is consistent with the axioms of Deductive Determinability and Functional Cognition. It underscores a nuanced perspective on cognitive capabilities, facilitating a deeper exploration of the nature of intelligence across a broad spectrum of systems.
