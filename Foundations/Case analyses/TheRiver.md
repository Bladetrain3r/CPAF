# The River

## A basic example of a system meeting the cognitive criteria for reflection.

Let's analyze a river ecosystem over the course of a few years using the foundational concepts of the Cognitive Progression Analysis Framework (CPAF) to test their practical applicability and determine if the ecosystem can be described as a "reflective" cognitive system capable of self-adaptation based on climate, interactions with other systems, and other factors.

### Entities:
The river ecosystem consists of various entities, such as water molecules, sediment particles, aquatic plants, fish, and other organisms. Each of these entities can initiate deviations in the system, such as a fish disturbing the sediment or a plant absorbing nutrients from the water.

### Information:
The river ecosystem constantly processes information, such as changes in water temperature, chemical composition, water flow rate, and the presence or absence of certain organisms. This information serves as the basis for deviations from the system's null state.

### Deviations:
Deviations in the river ecosystem can be observed as changes in water clarity, shifts in population dynamics, or alterations in the riverbed structure. These deviations are prompted by internal or external information processing, such as changes in seasonal precipitation or the introduction of pollutants.

### Interactions:
Interactions occur continuously within the river ecosystem, such as the exchange of nutrients between organisms, the transfer of energy through the food chain, or the displacement of sediment by water flow. These interactions lead to deviations in the system.

### Observation:
Observations in the river ecosystem are the deviations imposed by interactions, such as the change in water quality resulting from the decomposition of organic matter or the alteration of the riverbed due to erosion.

### System:
The river ecosystem is a complex system composed of numerous interacting entities that process information and undergo deviations to maintain or alter its state. The system's components work together to create a dynamic and responsive environment.

### Processes:
The river ecosystem undergoes various processes, such as nutrient cycling, sediment transport, and population growth or decline. Each process consists of a set of deviations that lead the system from one null state to another.

### Processing:
Processing in the river ecosystem occurs when information, such as changes in temperature or the availability of resources, is transformed into observable deviations, such as shifts in species composition or alterations in ecosystem productivity.

### Stimuli:
Stimuli in the river ecosystem can be external, such as changes in climate or the introduction of invasive species, or internal, such as the release of chemicals by organisms. These stimuli are intended to invoke deviations in the system, driving its adaptation and evolution.

### Experience:
The river ecosystem's experience is the ordered deviation of the system in response to stimuli over time. For example, the ecosystem may experience a sequence of deviations related to seasonal changes in water flow, temperature, and nutrient availability, which collectively shape its structure and function.

### Awareness and Memory:
The river ecosystem exhibits awareness and memory through its ability to register and store information about past experiences and deviations. This information is encoded in the system's physical structure, chemical composition, and biological communities, allowing it to "remember" and respond to previous conditions.

### Reflection:
Reflection in the river ecosystem occurs when the system accesses and considers its stored experiences and deviations to inform future responses. For example, the ecosystem may adjust its species composition or nutrient cycling processes based on past experiences with drought or flooding, demonstrating a form of self-adaptation.
