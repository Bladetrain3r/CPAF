## Foundational Concepts and Propositions

### Entity (E)
- **Proposition**: ∀e ∈ E, ∃d ∈ D | e can initiate d.
  - "For every entity in the set of all entities, there exists at least one deviation that the entity can initiate."

### Information (I)
- **Proposition**: ∀i ∈ I, ∃s ∈ S | s processes i → ∃d ∈ D.
  - "For every piece of information in the set of all information, there exists at least one system that processes this information, leading to at least one deviation."

### Deviation (D)
- **Proposition**: ∀d ∈ D, ∃s ∈ S, i ∈ I | s processes i → d is observed.
  - "For every deviation in the set of all deviations, there exists at least one system and one piece of information such that the system's processing of the information leads to an observed deviation."

### Interaction (Int)
- **Proposition**: ∀int ∈ Int, ∃i ∈ I, d ∈ D | int causes i → d.
  - "For every interaction in the set of all interactions, there exists at least one piece of information and one deviation such that the interaction causes the information to lead to the deviation."

### System (S)
- **Proposition**: ∀s ∈ S, ∃{e1, e2, ..., en} ⊆ E | s is composed of {e1, e2, ..., en} and ∃int ∈ Int | e1, e2, ..., en participate in int.
  - "For every system in the set of all systems, there exists a subset of entities that compose the system and at least one interaction in which these entities participate."

### Cognition (C)
- **Foundational Proposition**: Cognition is the process within a system characterized by deviations away from or towards a null state.
- **Peak Proposition**: Cognition at its peak is the synthesis of information into an autonomous and intrinsically motivated system capable of self-definition and adaptation.

### Null State (N)
- **Proposition**: ∀n ∈ N, ∃s ∈ S | n is the baseline state of s.
  - "For every null state in the set of all null states, there exists at least one system for which the null state is the baseline state."

### Stimuli (St)
- **Proposition**: ∀st ∈ St, ∃i ∈ I, s1, s2 ∈ S | s1 initiates i leading to a deviation in s2.
  - "For every stimulus in the set of all stimuli, there exists at least one piece of information and two systems such that one system initiates the information, leading to a deviation in the other system."




### Proposition: Entities and Deviations

- **Proposition**: ∀e ∈ E, ∃d ∈ D | e can initiate d.
  - "For every entity in the set of all entities, there exists at least one deviation that the entity can initiate."

### Proof by Contradiction: Existence of an Entity That Cannot Initiate a Deviation

1. **Assume the Contrary**:
   Assume there exists an entity \(e'\) within the set of all entities \(E\) such that \(e'\) cannot initiate any deviation, i.e., \( \nexists d \in D \) such that \(e'\) can initiate \(d\).

2. **Definition of an Entity**:
   Recall that an entity is defined as a unit capable of effecting or reflecting change, containing discrete information that can initiate deviations. This implies an intrinsic capacity for initiating change or deviation is a fundamental characteristic of being considered an entity within this framework.

3. **Contradiction**:
   The assumption that \(e'\) is an entity that cannot initiate any deviation directly contradicts the definition of an entity. By definition, for something to be considered an entity \(e\) in \(E\), it must have the capability, even if not actualized, to initiate a deviation \(d\) in \(D\).

4. **Conclusion**:
   Since assuming the existence of an entity that cannot initiate any deviation leads to a contradiction with the fundamental definition of what constitutes an entity, we conclude that our initial assumption is false. Therefore, it cannot be proven that there exists an entity that does not meet the criteria of being able to initiate at least one deviation.

This proof demonstrates that under the definitions and framework established for understanding entities and deviations, every entity, by virtue of being considered such, possesses the potential to initiate a deviation. The inability to initiate a deviation would invalidate the classification of something as an entity within this cognitive framework. Therefore, the original proposition stands validated through the logical impossibility of proving the existence of a contrary example.
